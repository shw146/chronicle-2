I particularly like the middle one with the weaponry, because it serves as a good reminder that even religious institutions like the Missions were established in places carved out with blood. It's a very stark reminder for us to look past the romanticized story that has been passed down through the years and see the foundations of what those stories were built on.

From the perspective of the sword: I woke up in a forge. The people around me talked about a new world and a king's support for its exploration. They were going to establish churches and convert the people from heathens into christians. I was put in a sheath by the one who made me, waiting for an owner to come. Months passed and finally a buyer came... a priest? Why would a religious figure like that need to buy a weapon? There wasn't much elaboration, things happened under the table, both literally and metaphorically. I wouldn't see the light of day for another few months while I sat in my owner's room on a ship headed for the new lands. I wasn't used much. I was left in my sheath most of the time, but there was a few cases where I did taste blood. It seemed like the native peoples didn't want to comply with my owner's desire for their conversion and out of frustration, stormed the building we stayed in. Only then did I feel the warm blood of these native people. Yet I was cast away soon after, my owner too ashamed to pick me back up. Centuries passed before I was recovered and placed into this glass box.

Quotes from the mission

These quotes of Mission San Diego de Alcalá reveals that the mission was far more than a simple religious outpost. It becomes clear that the mission also served as a refuge and center of survival for people attempting to establish themselves in unfamiliar lands.

While the sign says that there was no gold found on the premises of the church, there might be more to that story than we are being told. Spain had sponsored these mission not simply out of the goodness of their hearts, but rather because they were looking for something. The exorbitant amounts of resources they poured into these religious efforts had ulterior motives to them. Thus a plausible reason why gold was not found on the premises of the church could likely be because it was brought back to Spain in order to prove the value in the continued sponsorship of these missions.

A good clue into this is the living quarters in the church. For the most part, they are quite barren, only including the bare minimum needed to rest. However, assuming that the church had done its job and gave refuge to people in need, it would make much more sense for the mission to be adorned with various patchwork equipment used to accomodate for refugees. There would be signs of peoples' gratitude towards the mission in the form of quilts, clothing, wares, etc. Yet we don't see any of it. Perhaps it's just speculation, but it would make sense that these items would have been broken down and sent back to Spain where they would be sold for more conventional equipment.

Kumeyaay lands

Rather than drawing a map and potentially getting it wrong, I ended up finding one at the museum inside of the mission which dislays the regions that Native Americans lived in. I've also labeled them with the 1775 revolt at the Mission San Diego De Alcala and with the arrival of the Franciscans in 1769.

The arrival of the Franciscans marked an important start to a somewhat new but also repeated era of colonialization. Native tribes had already had to contend with the English on the east coast and had already had interactions with the French through their expeditions. We would also be naive to assume that they had not heard of the Spanish inquisition south of the them in modern day Mexico. Nonetheless, it was their first direct experience with colonialism, and clearly they didn't like it as virtue of the revolt in 1775 on the very Mission we visited.
